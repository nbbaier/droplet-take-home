Question 3 — Delivery guarantee: at-least-once, at-most-once, or "exactly-once"?

The assignment explicitly names "delivery guarantees" as a production concern, so the reviewer will look for a deliberate stance. This decision drives your queue design, your retry logic, and whether your Endpoints need to be idempotent.

The three options:

  • At-most-once — fire once, never retry. Simple, but you drop events on any failure. Bad fit for a service whose whole job is reliable delivery.
  • At-least-once — keep retrying until you get a success (or exhaust attempts). Events may be delivered more than once (e.g. the Endpoint received it but its 200 got lost). This is what essentially every real webhook system (Stripe, GitHub, Svix) provides.
  • Exactly-once — at-least-once delivery + dedup on the consumer side. You can't actually guarantee it end-to-end; you can only help consumers achieve it by sending a stable unique ID per delivery so they can dedup.

My recommendation: at-least-once, and make it explicit. State it plainly in the README. Then add the one thing that makes it production-grade: send a stable X-Delivery-Id (and/or X-Event-Id) header on every Attempt so consumers can dedup and approximate exactly-once themselves. That's a small touch that shows you understand the guarantee can't be exactly-once without consumer cooperation.

This implies: retries exist (next question), Deliveries have a terminal "exhausted/failed" state, and the same Delivery ID is stable across Attempts.

Do you want at-least-once with a stable delivery ID for consumer-side dedup, or are you aiming somewhere else?

Question 4 — Retry policy: what triggers a retry, how do you back off, and when do you give up?

At-least-once is only as good as its retry policy. Three sub-decisions here, and the reviewer will look for sensible defaults rather than novelty:

(a) What counts as a failure worth retrying? My rec: retry on connection errors, timeouts, and 5xx/429. Treat 2xx as success. Treat other 4xx (e.g. 400, 404, 410) as permanent failures — don't retry, mark the Delivery failed immediately (a malformed or gone Endpoint won't fix itself by retrying). A 410 Gone could even auto-disable the Endpoint, but that's future work.

(b) Backoff? My rec: exponential backoff with a small jitter, capped. Something like min(cap, base * 2^attempt) — e.g. base 1s, cap ~1h, so 1s, 2s, 4s, 8s… This is the industry norm and easy to defend.

(c) Max attempts / give-up? My rec: a small fixed cap — say 5 attempts — then the Delivery goes to a terminal failed state (your dead-letter, visible in the dashboard, ideally manually re-triggerable as a nice touch).

The tension for a 2–6h budget: real exponential backoff over hours means your test harness can't watch a retry succeed in a demo. So I'd recommend making the base delay and max attempts configurable, with tiny defaults in test/demo mode (e.g. base 100ms) so you can actually show a retry recovering, while documenting the production values.

Do you want that policy (retry 5xx/429/timeouts, exponential backoff+jitter, ~5 attempts, configurable delays), and should failed Deliveries be manually re-triggerable from the dashboard?

Good question — "what's retryable" is a genuine design space, not a single right answer. Here's the map.

The core axis is how you classify a response into {success, retry, give up}. Options, roughly from crude to refined:

1. Binary: 2xx = success, everything else = retry. Simplest. Any non-2xx (including connection errors/timeouts) retries until the cap. - Pro: trivial to implement and explain. - Con: you waste all 5 attempts retrying a 400 or 404 that will never succeed. A permanently misconfigured Endpoint churns your queue and delays its own terminal state by hours.

2. Three-way by class: 2xx = success, 4xx = permanent fail (no retry), 5xx/timeouts/connection errors = retry. This is the common sensible default. - Rationale: 4xx means "your request is wrong" — retrying the identical payload won't help. 5xx means "I'm broken right now" — might recover. - Con: it's slightly too aggressive on 4xx. Two 4xx codes are actually transient…

3. Three-way with 429 carved out (my earlier rec): like #2, but 429 Too Many Requests joins the retry set. - 429 is explicitly "try again later" — treating it as permanent would be wrong. Often you'd also honor a Retry-After header here. - This is where most production systems land as a baseline.

4. Fully explicit allow/deny lists with special cases. On top of #3: - 408 Request Timeout → retry (transient). - 409 Conflict → sometimes retry (depends on semantics). - Retry-After header (on 429 or 503) → override your backoff with the server's requested delay. - 410 Gone → not just permanent-fail but auto-disable the Endpoint (the consumer is telling you it's dead forever). - Redirects (3xx) → decide whether to follow or treat as misconfiguration (most webhook senders refuse to follow redirects, for security/SSRF reasons). - This is the most "real" but the most code.

The other dimension people forget: network-layer outcomes, independent of status code: - Connection refused / DNS failure / TLS error → retry (could be a deploy in progress). - Timeout → retry, but this is the dangerous one for at-least-once: the Endpoint may have processed the event and just been slow to ACK. That's exactly why the stable delivery-ID header from Q3 matters.

My recommendation stands at #3 as the baseline, with two cheap upgrades from #4 that punch above their weight: honor Retry-After on 429/503, and treat 410 Gone as permanent (mention auto-disable as future work). Everything else in #4 you can list as future work. That gives you a defensible, realistic story without burning your time budget on edge cases the harness won't even exercise.

Want to lock in #3-plus-those-two-upgrades, or keep it at a clean #3 and push all the special-casing to future work?

Question 5 — The queue and worker: what actually moves a Delivery from pending to delivered?

Your brain dump says "use a queue system" and "a worker loops through event_logs and delivers." Let's pin down the mechanism, because "queue system" spans a huge range and the wrong end of it will eat your whole time budget.

The options:

  • External broker (Redis/BullMQ, RabbitMQ, SQS). Real, but adds infra the reviewer has to stand up, and contradicts your single-binary/local-debuggable goal. Overkill for a take-home.
  • SQLite-as-queue + in-process worker (polling). Your deliveries table is the queue. A worker loop does SELECT ... WHERE status='pending' AND next_attempt_at <= now LIMIT n, delivers, updates status/next_attempt_at. Backoff is just a future timestamp. Survives restarts because it's all in the DB.
  • In-memory queue + in-process worker. Fastest to write, but loses everything on restart — directly undercuts the "persistence / delivery guarantees" you're selling.

My recommendation: SQLite-as-queue with an in-process polling worker. It's the sweet spot for this assignment — single bun process, no infra, fully persistent, and the retry backoff falls out naturally as a next_attempt_at column. It also makes your dashboard trivial (the queue is just a table you can SELECT). You poll on a short interval (say 100–500ms), claim a batch, deliver them (concurrently, with a cap), write results back.

The one thing to get right so it doesn't look naive: claiming. Even single-process with Bun's concurrency, you want to mark rows as processing (with an atomic UPDATE ... WHERE status='pending') so an in-flight Delivery isn't picked up twice by overlapping ticks, and so a crash mid-flight leaves a recoverable processing row (with a visibility timeout to reclaim stuck ones). This is the bit that shows you understand queue semantics.
